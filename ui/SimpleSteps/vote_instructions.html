Details and Examples

Here are some examples of tricky cases to help you make decisions:

Example 1. Terms can refer to multiple things.

term: Stanford
context: Stanford quarterback drafted to the NFL.

Question 1.1: Is "Stanford" likely referring to Stanford_University?
Answer: "Yes, definitely."

Question 1.2: Is "Stanford" likely referring to Stanford_Cardinal_football?
Answer: "Yes, definitely."

Example 2. Terms can have non-literal interpretations.

term: Capitol Hill
context: The bill will go to Capitol Hill today.

Question 2.1: Is "Capitol Hill" likely referring to Capitol_Hill?
Answer: "Yes, possibly."
Comment: Capitol Hill is really just a neighborhood of Washington D.C. (according to Wikipedia), so it's not a perfect reference for this context. If you wanted to say the answer was "No." that would also be a good answer.

Question 2.2: Is "Capitol Hill" likely referring to United_States_Congress?
Answer: "Yes, definitely."
Comment: In this context, "Capitol Hill" is a non-literal way of referring to Congress.

Example 3. The form of the word isn't that important.

term: Israeli
context: Israeli troops are on the move.

Question 3.1: Is "Israeli" likely referring to Israel?
Answer: "Yes, definitely."
Comment: Just because Israeli is an adjective, we still know it's referring to Israel.

Question 2.1: Is "Israeli" likely referring to Israel_Defense_Forces?
Answer: "Yes, definitely."
Comment: Again, terms can have multiple wikipedia references. Some are more literal than others.

Question 2.1: Is "Israeli" likely referring to Tel_Aviv?
Answer: "No."
Comment: Tel Aviv is in Israel, and Tel Aviv is has some association to Israel and this context, but if we focus on the term "Israeli" and see that Israel is not referring to Tel Aviv.

Example 4. It's okay to say that you're not sure.

term: President Bush
context: President Bush is from Texas

Question 4.1: Is "President Bush" likely referring to George_H._W._Bush?
Answer: "Unsure."
Comment: There's not enough information here to say which President Bush this is talking about, so just say that you're unsure.
